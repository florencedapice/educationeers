# Educationeers Website — Planning Guide

## How This Works

This document is the master guide for our planning process. We will work through it together over approximately **one week** before any code is written. Each section is a conversation — I will ask you the questions listed, you will answer them, and I will record your answers. Nothing gets assumed. Nothing gets invented. **If you haven't told me, it doesn't go in the website.**

This is intentional. The more detail you give me now, the closer the final website will be to exactly what you imagined.

**Rules of this process:**
- I will never skip ahead to building until every section is marked complete
- I will always ask follow-up questions if your answer raises new ones
- You can revisit and revise any section at any time
- When a section is done, I will update `WEBSITE_REFERENCE.md` with your answers so it stays current

## Step 1 — Site Identity & Name

**Goal:** Lock in the name of the website, what it's called everywhere, and how it presents itself.

**Questions to answer:**

1. What is the official name of the website? (The folder is called "educationeers" — is that the name, or is it something else entirely?)
2. Is the name one word, two words, or a phrase? How is it capitalized? (e.g., "Educationeers", "EducatioNEers", "The Educationeers", "educationeers")
3. Does the name have a tagline — a short phrase that appears next to or below the name that explains what the site is? If so, what is it? If not, would you like to come up with one?
4. Will there be a logo? Options:
   - Just the site name in a styled font (simplest)
   - A simple icon or symbol next to the text
   - A full illustrated logo you already have or will design
5. What is your domain name? (You mentioned you already own one — knowing this helps me make sure all links and deployment instructions are correct)
6. Is this website just for your class project, or do you want it to be something real that stays live after the semester? This matters for how much we invest in content.

## Step 2 — Mission & Messaging

**Goal:** Define the words and phrases the site uses to describe itself and speak to its audience. This affects every single page — headlines, button labels, section titles, and the general tone of the writing.

**Questions to answer:**

1. In one or two sentences, how would you describe this site to a teacher who has never heard of it? Write it naturally, the way you'd say it out loud to someone.
2. Who is the *primary* audience — the person you most want to land on this site and immediately feel like it was made for them? (You listed many audiences in your proposal — which one matters most?)
3. What do you want someone to feel when they land on the homepage? (Examples: inspired, relieved, informed, welcomed, motivated, validated)
4. What is the tone of the writing on the site? Pick the closest options:
   - Academic and formal vs. conversational and warm
   - Urgent and activist vs. calm and supportive
   - Broad and national vs. proudly local and regional
5. Are there any specific words or phrases that are important to use? (e.g., "nature-based," "problem-based," "Northern New England," specific acronyms)
6. Are there any words or phrases to avoid? (e.g., jargon that would alienate teachers, political language that might put off administrators)
7. Is there a short "elevator pitch" for the site — something like a mission statement — that you'd want displayed prominently?

**Questions to answer:**

## Step 4 — Navigation & Layout Shell

**Goal:** Define the elements that appear on every single page — the header, the navigation bar, and the footer. These are the bones of the site.

**Questions to answer:**

**Header / Navigation bar:**
1. What links should appear in the main navigation? List them in the order you want them to appear left to right.
2. Should the navigation bar stick to the top of the screen as you scroll down, or should it scroll away with the page?
3. On mobile phones, navigation menus usually collapse into a "hamburger" menu (three lines that open a dropdown). Is that okay, or do you have a different preference?
4. Should the site name/logo in the top left be a clickable link back to the homepage? (Almost always yes, but confirming.)
5. Is there anything else in the header — a search bar, a language toggle, a "submit your resource" button, social media icons?

**Footer (bottom of every page):**

6. What should be in the footer? Examples:
   - Copyright line and year
   - Your name or organization name
   - Navigation links (repeated)
   - Contact email
   - Social media links
   - A brief "about this site" blurb
   - A link to a submission/contribution form
7. What is the copyright holder for this site? (Your name? A project name? Your school?)

## Step 5 — Homepage

**Goal:** Define every section of the homepage, top to bottom, in order. The homepage is the most important page — it needs to immediately tell visitors what this is, why it matters, and where to go.

**Questions to answer:**

**Hero section (the very first thing you see):**
1. What is the main headline — the biggest, boldest text on the page? (Example: "Teaching Outdoors Starts Here" or "NNE's Hub for Problem-Based and Nature-Based Education")
2. Is there a subheadline — a sentence or two below the main headline that adds more detail?
3. Is there a call-to-action button in the hero? If so, what does it say and where does it link? (Example: "Explore Resources" → goes to resources page)
4. Is there a second button? (Sometimes sites have a primary CTA and a secondary one, e.g., "Explore Resources" and "Read the Research")
5. What is behind or next to the headline — a photo, an illustration, a color background, a video? If a photo or illustration, describe the mood and subject matter you imagine.

**Below the hero — what sections follow, in order?**

6. Should there be an "About / What is this site?" section — a short paragraph explaining the problem and solution? If yes, what does it say (in your own words)?
7. Should there be a section showing all five content areas (Research, Projects, Resources) as clickable cards that let people jump to each section?
8. Should there be a "Featured" section — highlighting one project, one resource, or one piece of research as a spotlight? Or is that too much to maintain?
9. Should there be a section aimed at a specific audience — e.g., a "For Teachers" block and a "For Organizations" block with different CTAs?
10. Should there be any statistics or facts — e.g., "X studies show..." or "Serving educators across Maine, Vermont, and New Hampshire"?
11. Should there be testimonials or quotes from real educators? (If yes, do you have any? If not, do you want placeholder ones?)
12. Should there be a section at the bottom of the homepage encouraging people to contribute or get involved?
13. List all the sections of the homepage in the order you want them, top to bottom.

## Step 15 — Deployment and Domain

**Goal:** Make sure the site can go live immediately after it's built, on your domain, for free.

**Questions to answer:**

2. Where did you buy your domain? (Examples: Namecheap, Google Domains, GoDaddy, Squarespace Domains) — This matters because the steps to connect it to a free host vary by registrar.

7. Do you want a simple guide included in the project folder explaining how to update the site after it's built — e.g., how to change text or add a new resource without coding knowledge?

## Reference Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| PBL | Problem-Based Learning — students learn by solving real-world, open-ended problems |
| NBE | Nature-Based Education — learning that takes place in or is inspired by the natural environment |
| NNE | Northern New England — Maine, New Hampshire, and Vermont |
| Static website | A website with no database or login system — all pages are pre-built files |
| Hero section | The first large section at the top of a homepage — usually a headline + image |
| CTA | Call to Action — a button or link that tells the user what to do next |
| Card | A box-shaped design element used to display one item (a study, a project, a resource) |
| Wireframe | A simple sketch or text description of a page layout, before design is applied |
| Palette | The set of colors used throughout a website |
| Typography | The fonts and text styling used throughout a website |
